2016 NHL Stanley Cup Results

An exercise in data visualization written in R.



In honor of the Stanley Cup playoffs starting in a few days, I was playing around with some hockey data I used in school just for fun. The dataset is found here.

It tells a story of the 2016 - 2017 NHL season, in which the Pittsburgh Penguins won the Stanley cup. What I found incredibly interesting when I started to analyze the data was the finding that there was a mild positive correlation between the number of giveaways per game and the number of games won overall. A giveaway is defined on the NHL's website as "a form of turnover where the player makes an unforced error that results in giving the puck up to the opposition." This finding is counter-intuitive. I would have thought that the more the team kept the puck, the more they would win. The scatterplot above seems to show the opposite.

In the scatterplot we have the average number of shots on goal per game versus the average number of goals per game. While all NHL teams are included here, only those teams that made the Stanley Cup playoffs have a visible team color. The size of the team's dot denotes the average number of giveaways per game.

Perhaps most striking from the shots on goal versus goals scatterplot are the St Louis Blue's figures. The Blues advanced to the third round of the Stanley Cup playoffs that year, despite their low shots on goal per game and their mediocre scoring. However, they have a significantly smaller rate of giveaways than nearly all of the other teams, especially out of those who made the playoffs. Our champions, the Pittsburgh Penguins averaged a whopping eight giveaways per game.

I wanted to know more about why the Blues' giveaways were so low but they failed to convert the time of possession into more goals. I looked at the goalie stats and found the average number of saves per game. What I found was that the Blues also had a comparatively low rate of goalie saves per game as well. (See below scatterplot.) This correlates with the low giveaway percentage.

A more thourough analysis of the dataset could provide further insights and answer other questions such as why Buffalo didn't make the playoffs despite their high save rate and their low giveaways.
